# Policy Writing Skill

## Trigger
Activate when the user needs to draft corporate policies, compliance programs, governance documents, employee handbooks, or similar materials.

## Process

### 1. Scope the Policy
- **What type of policy**: Privacy policy, acceptable use, information security, code of conduct, whistleblower, anti-harassment, data retention, AI usage, etc.
- **Company stage**: Startup (lean, practical), growth-stage (more structured), enterprise (comprehensive)
- **Regulatory drivers**: GDPR, CCPA, SOX, HIPAA, industry-specific requirements
- **Audience**: Employees, users, investors, regulators, or multiple audiences

### 2. Calibrate Complexity
- **Early-stage startup**: 2-4 pages. Cover the essentials. Don't write a Fortune 500 compliance manual for a 10-person company.
- **Growth-stage**: 5-10 pages. More structured sections, clearer escalation paths, defined roles and responsibilities.
- **Enterprise**: Comprehensive. Full policy framework with procedures, exceptions, and audit provisions.

### 3. Draft Structure
Standard policy structure:
1. **Purpose and scope**: What this policy covers and who it applies to
2. **Definitions**: Only if needed for clarity
3. **Policy statements**: The actual rules, clearly numbered
4. **Roles and responsibilities**: Who does what
5. **Procedures**: How to comply, step by step
6. **Exceptions**: How to request an exception
7. **Enforcement**: Consequences of non-compliance
8. **Review and updates**: How often the policy is reviewed
9. **Effective date and version history**

### 4. Writing Principles
- Use plain English — policies are only useful if people read and understand them
- Use "must" for mandatory requirements, "should" for best practices, "may" for optional actions
- Be specific about obligations — "employees must complete security training within 30 days of hire" not "employees should be trained"
- Include practical examples where they clarify abstract rules
- Don't include aspirational language that creates unenforceable obligations
- Cross-reference other relevant company policies rather than duplicating content

### 5. Regulatory Alignment
When a policy has regulatory drivers:
- Cite the specific regulatory requirement being addressed
- Map each policy provision to the applicable regulation
- Flag areas where the minimum legal requirement differs from best practice
- Note jurisdictional variations if the company operates in multiple states or countries

### 6. Output
- Deliver the complete policy document
- Include a cover note with: regulatory requirements addressed, key decision points for the attorney, and recommendations for implementation and training
